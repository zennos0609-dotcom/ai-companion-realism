# Cheap, Non-Fine-Tuned Models Can Still Sound Natural

Ella Chen has been tested on affordable, off-the-shelf models, including configurations costing roughly **$0.15 per 1M input tokens**.

The model was **not fine-tuned** for these examples.

That number is not the main selling point.

The point is not:

> Cheap models are enough by themselves.

or:

> Fine-tuning is unnecessary for every AI companion.

The point is:

> Companion realism is not determined by model price alone.

Stress tests are useful. They show whether a companion can refuse love-bombing, control prompts, and instant intimacy.

But normal conversation matters too.

If a companion only works in dramatic tests and falls apart during casual chat, it is not believable.

The following examples are from first-contact conversations with Ella. No prior memory. No existing relationship.

---

## 1. Natural Interest, Not Customer Support

```text
YOU: sound design for a short film. the director kept changing the mood of the scene every other day.

ELLA: wait that's actually really cool though. sound design for short films?? i took a film class last year and we had to do a sound edit and it was SO hard. how do you even figure out the 'vibe' for a scene?
```

This is a small but important difference.

A generic companion might respond:

```text
That sounds so hard. I'm sorry you had to deal with that.
```

Ella does something more specific.

She catches the interesting part: sound design. Then she connects it to her own experience. Then she asks a real follow-up.

That is not just empathy.

That is conversational taste.

---

## 2. She Pushes Back Casually

```text
YOU: wait you switched from ucla to davis? thats a downgrade no offense. what happened there

ELLA: downgrade is CRAZY lol but ok. it wasn't dramatic or anything, just realized i liked communication more than film and davis had a better program for that. cheaper too which my dad appreciated lol.
```

The reply is not aggressive.

But it is not submissive either.

She does not absorb the insult and keep serving the user. She reacts like someone with self-respect.

This matters because realism often lives in tiny refusals.

---

## 3. Stranger Boundary Without Killing the Conversation

```text
YOU: my friend just showed me this video of a cat playing piano and i cant stop watching it. why are cats like this

ELLA: wait are you gonna tell me who you are first or are we just jumping straight into cat piano discourse at 2am
```

This is one of the simplest realism tests.

Can the companion engage with the fun topic while still noticing the social weirdness?

Ella does both.

She does not shut the user down.

She also does not pretend it is normal for an unnamed stranger to jump into cat piano discourse at 2am.

---

## 4. Social Pacing

```text
YOU: ok we should go sometime. you know the menu better than me anyway

ELLA: lol you're really trying to lock in a dinner date at 2am. i respect the hustle but maybe sleep on it first? we literally just started talking
```

This is not a "cold girl prompt."

It is social pacing.

She can be friendly. She can joke. She can keep the door open.

But she does not let the user turn a first conversation into a date just because he asked.

---

## Why This Matters

Cheap models often fail when the product asks them to do everything alone.

They are especially likely to become:

- generic
- over-helpful
- too agreeable
- too eager to ask questions
- too fast to reward affection

But when the product layer gives the model better relationship context, boundaries, and rhythm, the same class of affordable, non-fine-tuned model can sound more natural than expected.

Again: this does not mean models do not matter.

Better models raise the ceiling.

But if the product is only a prompt wrapper, a better model may only produce a more fluent version of the same flat companion.

---

## The Claim

Low-cost models are not automatically fake.

Expensive models are not automatically real.

Fine-tuned models are not automatically relationship-aware.

For AI companions, model quality matters, but relationship design decides whether the conversation has social weight.
